Treaty 9: Key Moments and Players

A snapshot of the treaty's history, the negotiation process, and the early gap between what was written and what was promised.

Key Events in Treaty 9 History

Timeline of Treaty 9 Events
Year Event Description
1905 Treaty 9 Negotiations Begin Government commissioners travelled by canoe to remote northern posts to meet Cree and Anishinaabe leaders.
1905 Adhesions Added Additional First Nations joined the agreement after the original signing.
1906 Final Signing The treaty was signed at several Hudson's Bay Company posts, including Osnaburgh House.
1906–1920 Promised Supports Delayed The treaty's commitments were often delayed or not delivered as expected.
1929–1930 Territorial Accessions Further land was added to the treaty's coverage, extending it further into the north.

Where the Treaty Was Signed

  • Moose Factory On the James Bay coast; one of the first signing locations.
  • Fort Albany A Hudson's Bay Company post along the Albany River.
  • Fort Hope Located in Eabametoong territory.
  • Osnaburgh House Located in Mishkeegogamang territory; site of the final 1906 signing.

A Unilateral Process

Indigenous Nations

Leaders from the Omushkegowuk (Cree), Anishinaabe (Ojibwe), and Anishininimowin (Oji-Cree) nations met with commissioners at posts across the territory shown above.

Government Commissioners

Crown representatives arrived with a pre-written agreement that left no room for Indigenous leaders to change its terms, prioritizing quick signatures over genuine negotiation.

Written Text vs. Oral Promises

The gap between the Crown's written terms and the promises Indigenous leaders understood orally
Category Written Terms (Crown) Oral Promises (Indigenous Nations)
Sovereignty Territory placed under exclusive federal jurisdiction. Full autonomy and traditional way of life, without interference.
Land Rights "Cession" of hundreds of thousands of square miles. Peaceful coexistence and sharing of the land with newcomers.
Reserves 1 square mile (640 acres) allotted per family of five. Ability to remain on ancestral lands without restriction.
Subsistence Hunting, fishing, and trapping permitted, subject to government regulation. An inalienable right to hunt, fish, and trap anywhere, as in the past.

The Breaking Point

The clause allowing subsistence rights to be "subject to regulation" became the real point of contention. Commissioners orally assured leaders that their way of life would not change, while the written text quietly gave the government legal power to restrict it. For many Elders, the spoken word — not the page — is the true treaty, because it is the basis on which their authority was understood.
