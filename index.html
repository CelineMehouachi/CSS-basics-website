Treaty 9 / The James Bay Treaty, 1905–1906

First Nations Treaty 9

Treaty 9 is one of the largest and most complex of Canada's numbered treaties, covering Northern Ontario and Northwestern Quebec. At its centre lies a major gap between the written text signed by the Crown and the oral promises made to the Omushkegowuk (Cree), Anishinaabe (Ojibwe), and Anishininimowin (Oji-Cree) nations — a gap that still shapes legal and political relations in Canada today. This site explores the history, the broken promises, and the path toward reconciliation.

A Treaty Timeline

  • 1905 Negotiations Begin Commissioners travel by canoe to remote northern posts to meet Cree and Anishinaabe leaders.
  • 1906 Final Signing The treaty is signed at Hudson's Bay Company posts including Osnaburgh House.
  • 1906–1920 Promised Supports Delayed Many of the treaty's commitments are delayed or never fully delivered.
  • 1929–1930 Territorial Accessions Further land is added to the treaty's coverage, extending it deeper into the north.
  • Today A Living Treaty Courts and Indigenous nations continue to define what the treaty's promises mean in practice.

Why Treaty 9 Matters

Two Perspectives

For the Crown, Treaty 9 was a tool for transferring sovereignty over a vast territory. For the Indigenous nations who signed it, it represented a pact of peaceful coexistence and land sharing — a disagreement that was never resolved.

Key Topics

  • A rushed, pre-written negotiation process
  • Written terms versus oral promises
  • Subsistence rights and the "regulation" clause
  • Ongoing legal recognition of oral histories

A Living Treaty

The Nishnawbe Aski Nation continues to assert that sovereignty was never ceded, and Canadian courts increasingly rely on oral histories to define the duty to consult before development on treaty land.
