Moving Forward with Treaty 9

Far from a historical artifact, Treaty 9 remains a living legal and political process — one that continues to shape resource development, land rights, and the recognition of Indigenous voices across Northern Canada. Today, we look at how the treaty's original spirit is being revitalized through modern legal challenges and community-led initiatives.

Honouring Our Treaties

Produced by the Nishnawbe Aski Nation, this video features Grand Chief Derek Fox and Deputy Grand Chief Bobby Narcisse on why treaty education matters today.

Why Treaty 9 Is Still a Living Issue

Resource Development

Treaty 9 was originally designed to open the region to mining, forestry, and hydroelectric development. These same industries remain at the centre of territorial tensions today.

Land Rights and Sovereignty

Organizations such as the Nishnawbe Aski Nation continue to challenge the restrictive, written interpretation of the treaty, asserting that sovereignty over their lands was never ceded.

Duty to Consult

Courts increasingly rely on oral histories to require the government to consult Indigenous nations before industrial projects, recognizing that the promises made in 1905 created lasting obligations.

Legitimacy of Oral Histories

Treaty 9 is a key example of Canadian law reconciling written text with the oral histories of Elders, which are now recognized as crucial evidence in interpreting the treaty's true meaning.

Key Takeaways

  • An imposed, not negotiated, process — Treaty 9 was presented to the Omushkegowuk, Anishinaabe, and Anishininimowin nations as a pre-written, non-negotiable document.
  • The primacy of oral promises — for Indigenous peoples, the oral promise to hunt and fish "everywhere" forms the true core of the agreement, while the written "regulation" clauses are seen as a breach of trust.
  • A living legal instrument — Treaty 9 continues to shape negotiations over resource access and the recognition of Indigenous rights in the North.

Final Thought

Understanding Treaty 9 means recognizing that Canada's history is not written solely on paper, but also lives in the oral memory of the peoples who have inhabited the land for millennia.
